# Sources — Paddock content authoring

Canonical source-tier list for content authoring across the per-series literacy tabs. Compiled from operator research, 2026-05-19.

**General rule:** Prefer Tier 0 / 1 / 3 / 4 over Tier 2 / 5 / 6 over Wikipedia. Wikipedia is acceptable as a last resort but flag for replacement in subsequent source-refinement passes.

**Citation format in drafts:**

```
[^1]: [Author or Title], [Source], accessed DD Month YYYY, [URL].
```

For Tier 0 books, cite by ISBN. Add Internet Archive URL where freely accessible.

## Tier 0 — Print canon

The books that the upstream web sources themselves defer to.

### 29. Doug Nye — *The Autocourse History of the Grand Prix Car*

Two-volume set, the closest thing to a single authoritative reference for postwar GP-car engineering and competitive narrative.

- **Vol 1: 1945–1965** (Hazleton Publishing, 1993, ISBN 978-1874557500). Post-war formula through the early rear-engined revolution.
- **Vol 2: 1966–1991** (Hazleton Publishing, expanded edition of the 1986 original; ISBN 978-0905138947). Repco Brabham, McLaren M23, Lotus 78 & 79, Ferrari V12s, Williams FW07, McLaren TAG MP4/2s, etc. Structured by technical inflection points rather than chronologically — exactly the model for History-tab framing.
- Vol 2 free via Internet Archive controlled-borrow (1-hour windows): https://archive.org/details/autocoursehistor0000nyed.
- Vol 1 and the expanded Vol 2 readily available used on AbeBooks / eBay (~£20–60).

**Use for:** the causal/technical chain of how Grand Prix cars actually evolved. The single most quotable book for engineering-driven turning points without mythologising.

### 30. Mike Lang — *Grand Prix!*

Four-volume race-by-race chronicle of the World Championship 1950–1984 (Foulis/Haynes).

- Vol 1: 1950–1965 (1981, ISBN 0854292764). Foreword by Stirling Moss.
- Vol 2: 1966–1973 (1982). Foreword by Jackie Stewart.
- Vol 3: 1974–1980.
- Vol 4: 1981–1984.
- Combined Vol 1+2 edition (1990, ISBN 0854298614) covers 1950–1973 in one book.
- Series stopped at 1984; *Autocourse* annuals fill the 1985-onward gap.

**Use for:** contemporaneous narrative accounts of every World Championship race 1950–1984.

### 31. Laurence Pomeroy — *The Grand Prix Car*

Two-volume engineering history; the foundational technical text for pre-1955 Grand Prix cars.

- Vol 1: 1906–1939 (Motor Racing Publications, originally 1949; revised 1954). 267 pages. 17 representative GP cars from the 1908 Itala to the 1939 Mercedes-Benz W163.
- Vol 2: 1947–1954 (Motor Racing Publications/Temple Press, 1955). 344 pages. Tipo 158 Alfa Romeo through 1954 Mercedes W196.

Pomeroy was Technical Editor of *The Motor* 1936–1958 — contemporaneous, not retrospective.

**Use for:** pre-1955 technical history. The only era where Pomeroy is the primary source rather than secondary.

## Tier 1 — Primary / governing authorities

### 1. formula1.com — https://www.formula1.com

The series' own narrative. Use for canonical framings the sport endorses (championship structure, official era markers, season-by-season official results) and current technical reg announcements. Treat their long-form historical features as marketing-shaped, not neutral.

### 2. FIA archives — https://www.fia.com/f1-archives

Regulatory primary source. Founding/governance facts, official rule-change dates, FIA presidency timeline. Sparse but unimpeachable for what it does contain.

### 3. Ferrari Archive (Maranello)

- https://www.ferrari.com/en-EN/magazine/articles/the-ferrari-archive
- https://www.ferrari.com/en-EN/museums/ferrari-maranello

Team-side primary documentation: founding-era correspondence, model lineage, racing director records back to 1947.

### 4. Williams Heritage — https://www.williamsf1.com/heritage

Authoritative on Williams' chassis lineage and the Patrick Head era framing.

### 5. International Motor Racing Research Center (IMRRC) — https://www.racingarchives.org

Watkins Glen-based formal archive. ~5,000 books, 250 periodical titles, journalist papers, oral histories, race programs. The closest thing to a neutral academic motorsport library; email requests get answered.

**Use for:** verification on obscure or contested claims.

## Tier 2 — Specialist journalism / expert analysis

### 6. Motor Sport Magazine archive — https://www.motorsportmagazine.com/archive/issues/

Searchable back catalogue of race reports, interviews and features going back to 1924. The crown jewel. Paywalled (~$8/mo) but contains contemporary race reports for every era.

**Use for:** primary-source race weekend reporting from any decade.

### 7. Motor Sport Database — https://www.motorsportmagazine.com/database/series/f1/

The same publisher's reference database covering nearly every motorsport race since 1894.

### 8. Nigel Roebuck (Motor Sport) — https://www.motorsportmagazine.com/articles/author/nigel-roebuck

Roebuck's "Reflections" column and the multi-year "Legends" series (Hunt, Williams, Andretti, Nuvolari, etc.) are the canonical long-form retrospective writing on individual figures.

**Use for:** biographical depth and paddock-context detail no statistical source captures.

### 9. Mark Hughes at The Race — https://the-race.com/author/mark-hughes/

Technical-meets-narrative analysis. Engineering causation behind competitive outcomes from ~2000 onward.

### 10. The Race (general) — https://the-race.com

Inherited a lot of the Autosport ex-staff DNA; long-form analysis tier above current-events outlets.

**Use for:** post-2019 era explanations.

### 11. Autosport — https://www.autosport.com

The British weekly, founded 1950. Long-form features and historical pieces; main news flow is current-affairs.

**Notable feature:** the "From the Archive" series of retrospective long-form pieces (e.g. Maurice Hamilton on Spygate 2007).

### 12. Atlas F1 archive — https://archive.atlasf1.com/search/dir.html

Frozen archive of the Atlas F1 e-magazine 1995–2005. Contemporaneous coverage of the Schumacher-era, with feature columns and technical analysis from the period.

**Use for:** how a thing was understood at the time, rather than how history later framed it.

### 13. Joe Saward — JoeBlogsF1 — https://joesaward.wordpress.com

Saward has attended every Grand Prix since 1988. F1 politics / business / history from inside the paddock.

**Use for:** the political and commercial history (Ecclestone era, Concorde Agreements, FOTA–FIA disputes, Spygate / Crashgate context). Specific posts to know about: "Au revoir, Renault" (2010-12-08) on Crashgate; "The end of Stepneygate" (2010-10-04) on the Spygate aftermath.

### 14. GrandPrix.com (Inside F1) — https://www.grandprix.com

Saward-co-founded. Hosts the Grand Prix Encyclopedia (race-by-race reports, full driver bios, complete team histories) plus the Cahier photographic archive going back to the 1950s.

**Use for:** per-race and per-driver reference.

### 15. Craig Scarborough (Scarbs)

- https://scarbsf1.com
- Scarbs columns at https://motorsport.tech

F1 technical-regulation journalism.

**Use for:** accurate technical-reg evolution (active suspension, ground effect, hybrid PU mechanics).

## Tier 3 — Statistical / data-of-record

### 16. StatsF1 — https://www.statsf1.com

The cleanest pan-history statistical reference. All results and statistics on Formula 1 since its creation.

**Use for:** any *who, when, where, what time* verification.

### 17. ChicaneF1 — https://www.chicanef1.com

Independent stats site, often catches data the bigger sites miss.

**Use for:** cross-check on StatsF1.

### 18. Ergast Developer API — https://ergast.com/mrd/

Programmatic access to historical Formula 1 data including race results, driver information, circuit information.

**Use for:** if Paddock ever embeds verified stats directly rather than copy-paste.

### 19. 4mula1stats — https://www.4mula1stats.com

Another stats cross-reference.

**Use for:** when StatsF1 and ChicaneF1 disagree.

## Tier 4 — Specialist deep history

### 20. 8W (Forix, hosted by Autosport)

- https://forix.com/8w/
- https://8w.forix.com

The single best F1-history site on the internet for **why** things happened. Run by Mattijs Diepraam. Standout sections:

- "Defining moments in Grand Prix history": https://forix.com/8w/defining-moments.html — exactly the causal-not-chronological framing the History tab needs.
- "Identity changes in Grand Prix racing": https://8w.forix.com/secondlife.html — disambiguates team-lineage confusion (which Lotus is which, when Sauber became BMW).
- "Truth or thereabouts" — myth-busting against commonly-repeated F1 history errors.
- "How the FOCA became the new FIA" — FISA-FOCA war and Concorde Agreement primary narrative.
- "Seasons' portraits": https://forix.autosport.com/8w/when.html — season-by-season interpretive essays.
- 8W Sources page: https://forix.autosport.com/8w/sources.html — upstream archives (Allen Brown, Dennis David, GEL Motorsport Information, Memoire des Stands, etc.).

### 21. The Golden Era of Grand Prix Racing (Leif Snellman) — https://kolumbus.fi/leif.snellman/main.htm

The authoritative web source for pre-war Grand Prix racing 1934–40.

**Use for:** any reference to F1's pre-history — AIACR-era European Championship, Mercedes-Auto Union era, the formula's lineage before 1946.

### 22. Hans Etzrodt's "Black Books" / Grand Prix winners 1894–present (Forix 6th Gear) — https://8w.forix.com/6thgear/gp-yby.html

Race-by-race results going back to the 1894 Paris-Rouen, with the (AIACR) Grande Épreuve / World Championship marker.

**Use for:** the championship-and-non-championship distinction that's badly mangled on Wikipedia.

### 23. OldRacingCars.com (Allen Brown) — https://www.oldracingcars.com

Chassis-by-chassis history with race entries.

**Use for:** verifying which exact car competed where — critical when the historical narrative makes claims about specific machines.

## Tier 5 — Practitioner / journalist communities

Cite their conclusions, not raw forum posts.

### 24. Autosport Forums — Nostalgia Forum — https://forums.autosport.com/forum/22-the-nostalgia-forum/

The single most valuable community on the internet for F1 historical fact-checking. Legitimate motorsport journalists, historians, and hardcore fans contribute. Use carefully — direct quotation is risky; cite conclusions reached in long threads.

### 25. Autosport Forums — Technical Forum Archive — https://forums.autosport.com/forum/107-the-technical-forum-archive/

Same logic for technical-regulation history; engineers and former team members contribute.

## Tier 6 — Long-form video / documentary

Tone / framing reference, not direct citation.

### 26. The Race (YouTube) — https://www.youtube.com/@TheRaceMedia

Edd Straw and Mark Hughes appear here; long-form analysis pieces are citation-grade.

### 27. Aldas (YouTube) — https://www.youtube.com/c/AldasBusiness

Long-form retrospective video essays on specific drivers / teams / seasons.

**Use for:** tone reference — Aldas does encyclopedic-narrative voice well.

### 28. Chain Bear (Stuart Taylor) (YouTube)

Explainers on technical evolution and rule changes.

**Use for:** sanity check that complex tech history can be explained in plain English.

## Format conventions

### URL citation

```
[^N]: Title, Source, accessed DD Month YYYY, URL.
```

### Book citation (ISBN-only, no URL)

```
[^N]: Author, *Title*, Publisher, Year, ISBN ###-#######.
```

### Book citation with Internet Archive

```
[^N]: Author, *Title*, Publisher, Year, ISBN ###-#######; Internet Archive (controlled-borrow), URL.
```

### Mixed source for a single claim

```
[^N]: Primary source A, accessed DD Month YYYY, URL-A; Secondary source B for additional detail, URL-B.
```

### Specific article URL when known

Always prefer specific article URLs over landing pages. Flag where only landing pages are cited:

```
[^N]: Source, Database landing page, accessed DD Month YYYY, URL. [Specific article URL to be located in source-refinement pass.]
```
