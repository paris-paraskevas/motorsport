## Origin

Formula One is the Fédération Internationale de l'Automobile's premier single-seater championship.[^1] The FIA was founded in 1904 as the AIACR and renamed after the Second World War.[^1] It inaugurated the World Drivers' Championship in 1950; the first race ran at Silverstone on 13 May 1950 and was won by Giuseppe Farina for Alfa Romeo.[^1][^2] Juan Manuel Fangio took the next five titles between 1951 and 1957 for four different manufacturers — Alfa Romeo, Maserati, Mercedes-Benz, and Ferrari — setting the early benchmark.[^3] The World Constructors' Championship was introduced in 1958; Ferrari has competed in every season since 1950, a record held by no other team.[^4]

## Turning points

### Technical revolutions

The modern car's architecture emerged in the late 1950s and early 1960s. Cooper proved the rear-engined layout with Jack Brabham in 1959 and 1960; Lotus's Type 25 of 1962, designed by Colin Chapman, introduced the monocoque chassis as the new standard.[^5] Ground-effect aerodynamics arrived with the Lotus 78 in 1977, and the turbocharged engine with the Renault RS01 the same year. The current 1.6-litre V6 hybrid power-unit era began in 2014; ground-effect aerodynamics returned in 2022 after a 39-year absence.[^6]

### Safety reform

Modern safety regulation traces back to Jim Clark's death at a Formula Two race in 1968.[^7] Jackie Stewart's driver-led campaign through the early 1970s extended it: crash barriers, seat belts, full-face helmets, and the abandonment of unsafe circuits including the Nürburgring's 22-kilometre Nordschleife layout after Niki Lauda's near-fatal 1976 crash.[^7] The deaths of Roland Ratzenberger and Ayrton Senna at Imola in 1994 forced the most extensive overhaul in the sport's history — stricter crash structures, the HANS head and neck support device, and structural changes to monocoque survival cells.[^8]

### Contested championships

The championship has a recurring tradition of title finales decided by controversy. Prost and Senna collided at Suzuka in 1989 (Prost took the title after Senna was disqualified) and again in 1990 (Senna deliberately drove into Prost on lap one to take it back).[^9] Schumacher collided with Hill at Adelaide in 1994 to take his first title and was disqualified from the 1997 standings after a deliberate collision with Villeneuve at Jerez.[^10] The 2021 Abu Dhabi finale saw race director Michael Masi removed after applying the safety-car procedure inconsistently on the final restart, allowing Verstappen to take his first title from Hamilton.[^11] Off-track integrity scandals have included Spygate (2007, McLaren fined one hundred million US dollars and excluded from the Constructors' Championship) and Crashgate (2008, Renault ordered Nelson Piquet Jr. to crash at the Singapore Grand Prix; team principal Flavio Briatore received a lifetime FIA ban, later partially lifted).[^12]

## Today's shape

The current calendar comprises twenty-four rounds.[^13] Schumacher and Hamilton hold the all-time record for World Drivers' Championships at seven each; Hamilton holds the career race-win record at 105.[^3] Verstappen won four consecutive titles from 2021 to 2024 before Lando Norris took the 2025 Drivers' Championship for McLaren, which retained the Constructors' title from 2024.[^14] Ferrari leads the all-time Constructors' count with 16 titles, followed by McLaren (10), Williams (9), and Mercedes (8, consecutive 2014–2021).[^4][^3] The 2026 regulations close the ground-effect era, retire the drag-reduction system in favour of active aerodynamics, raise the electrical share of total power to roughly fifty per cent, mandate fully sustainable fuels, and introduce a Manual Override Mode for overtaking.[^15]

# Alternate: long-form decade-by-decade (draft-3, preserved verbatim)

This longer form remains useful for driver pages, team pages, season-recap blog posts, or any future deep-history surface. Not in current production; not loaded by `HistoryTab.tsx`.

Formula One is the premier class of single-seater motor racing, governed by the Fédération Internationale de l'Automobile (FIA), founded on 20 June 1904 as the Association Internationale des Automobile Clubs Reconnus (AIACR) and renamed following the resumption of motorsport after the Second World War.[^a1] The annual World Championship is contested across two parallel titles — the World Drivers' Championship, first awarded in 1950, and the World Constructors' Championship, first awarded in 1958.[^a2]

## 1950s

The inaugural Drivers' Championship season comprised seven rounds; the opening race was held at Silverstone on 13 May 1950 and won by Giuseppe Farina for Alfa Romeo.[^a3] The decade was contested largely by European manufacturers — Alfa Romeo, Ferrari, Maserati, and Mercedes-Benz — with Juan Manuel Fangio winning five Drivers' Championships between 1951 and 1957 for four different teams: Alfa Romeo (1951), Maserati (1954, 1957), Mercedes-Benz (1954, 1955), and Ferrari (1956).[^a4] Ferrari has competed in every championship season since 1950, a distinction held by no other team.[^a5]

## 1960s

Cooper pioneered the move from front- to rear-engined cars in the late 1950s, winning back-to-back Drivers' Championships with Jack Brabham in 1959 and 1960.[^a6] Lotus, under designer Colin Chapman, developed the layout further: the Lotus 18 of 1960 was the team's first mid-engined chassis,[^a7] and the Lotus 25 of 1962 introduced the first fully stressed monocoque chassis in Formula One.[^a8] Jim Clark, driving for Lotus, won Drivers' Championships in 1963 and 1965;[^a9] at the time of his death in a Formula Two race in 1968 he held the records for most wins, most podium finishes, and most pole positions.[^a10]

## 1970s

Jackie Stewart won three Drivers' Championships with Tyrrell in 1969, 1971, and 1973, and campaigned across his career for crash barriers, seat belts, full-face helmets, and the boycott of unsafe circuits, including Spa-Francorchamps and the Nürburgring.[^a11] Niki Lauda's near-fatal crash at the Nürburgring in 1976 ended championship use of the original 22-kilometre Nordschleife layout. Lotus introduced ground-effect aerodynamics with the Lotus 78 in 1977; Renault introduced the turbocharged engine to Formula One the same year, beginning the technical era that defined the following decade.

## 1980s

Williams won back-to-back Constructors' Championships in 1980 and 1981 with Ford-Cosworth power, and again in 1986 and 1987 with Honda engines, with Nelson Piquet taking the 1987 Drivers' Championship.[^a12] From 1988 the McLaren-Honda partnership achieved one of the most concentrated dominance cycles in the sport's history, winning fifteen of sixteen races in 1988 with teammates Alain Prost and Ayrton Senna, and claiming four consecutive Constructors' Championships from 1988 to 1991.[^a13] The Prost-Senna rivalry came to a head at the 1989 Japanese Grand Prix at Suzuka: the two collided while disputing the lead, and after Senna recovered to take the chequered flag he was disqualified for missing a chicane on his return to the track, handing Prost the Drivers' Championship.[^a14] Ground-effect aerodynamics had been banned by the FIA in 1983.[^a15]

## 1990s

The rivalry resumed at Suzuka the following year: at the 1990 Japanese Grand Prix, Senna — now the sole title contender at McLaren after Prost's move to Ferrari — drove into Prost at the first corner of the opening lap, taking both out and clinching his second Drivers' Championship in what he later acknowledged was a deliberate retaliation.[^a14] Williams returned to the front of the field with Renault power, winning Constructors' Championships in 1992, 1993, 1994, 1996, and 1997, with Drivers' titles for Nigel Mansell (1992), Alain Prost (1993), Damon Hill (1996), and Jacques Villeneuve (1997).[^a12] The 1994 San Marino Grand Prix at Imola was marked by the deaths of Roland Ratzenberger in qualifying on 30 April and Ayrton Senna in the race on 1 May; the accidents triggered the most extensive safety reforms in the sport's history, including stricter crash-test requirements, the introduction of the HANS head and neck support device, and structural improvements to monocoque survival cells.[^a16] Michael Schumacher won his first two Drivers' Championships with Benetton in 1994 and 1995; both championship pursuits ended in title-decider collisions — at the 1994 Australian Grand Prix in Adelaide he collided with Damon Hill while leading the championship by a single point, taking both out and clinching the title,[^a17] and at the 1997 European Grand Prix at Jerez he turned into Jacques Villeneuve in a further attempted title decider, was found by the FIA to have acted deliberately, and was disqualified from the championship standings for that season, though his individual race results were retained.[^a18]

## 2000s

Ferrari with Schumacher dominated the early 2000s, winning six consecutive Constructors' Championships from 1999 to 2004 and five consecutive Drivers' Championships from 2000 to 2004 — the longest unbroken run of Drivers' titles in F1 history at the time, contributing to Schumacher's career totals of seven World Drivers' Championships and 91 race wins, both records at his retirement.[^a5][^a9] Renault and Fernando Alonso interrupted Ferrari's run with consecutive Drivers' Championships in 2005 and 2006.[^a9] The 2007 season was overshadowed by the "Spygate" affair, in which McLaren was found in possession of confidential Ferrari technical information; the FIA fined the team one hundred million US dollars, the largest penalty in motorsport history, and excluded McLaren from the Constructors' Championship, though its drivers Fernando Alonso and Lewis Hamilton retained their points-based standings.[^a19] Hamilton won the Drivers' Championship in 2008 with McLaren by a single point over Felipe Massa.[^a13] The 2008 season was itself later overshadowed by the "Crashgate" scandal at the Singapore Grand Prix, in which Renault was found to have instructed Nelson Piquet Jr. to crash deliberately to bring out a safety car at a moment favourable to teammate Alonso, who won the race; team principal Flavio Briatore received a lifetime ban from FIA-sanctioned competition (later partially lifted by a French court).[^a20] The decade closed with the one-season Brawn GP team winning both championships in 2009 with Jenson Button.

## 2010s

Red Bull Racing with Sebastian Vettel won four consecutive Constructors' and Drivers' Championships from 2010 to 2013.[^a9] The current technical era began in 2014 with the introduction of 1.6-litre V6 turbocharged hybrid power units incorporating kinetic-energy and exhaust-heat recovery systems.[^a21] Mercedes adapted most effectively to the new regulations, winning eight consecutive Constructors' Championships from 2014 to 2021 — the longest unbroken run in the championship's history.[^a2] Hamilton claimed six Drivers' Championships during the decade (2014, 2015, 2017, 2018, 2019), with teammate Nico Rosberg taking the title in 2016 before retiring at year's end.[^a9]

## 2020s

Hamilton won a seventh Drivers' Championship in 2020, equalling Schumacher's all-time record, and overtook Schumacher's career race-win total the same season.[^a9] The 2021 season ended in controversy at the Abu Dhabi season finale, where race director Michael Masi authorised an unusual safety-car restart procedure that left Max Verstappen on fresh tyres immediately behind Lewis Hamilton on the final lap; Verstappen passed Hamilton to take his first Drivers' Championship, and the FIA subsequently removed Masi from the race-director role and amended the safety-car regulations for 2022.[^a22] Verstappen went on to win four consecutive Drivers' Championships from 2021 to 2024.[^a9] Ground-effect aerodynamics returned in 2022 for the first time since 1983.[^a15] McLaren regained the Constructors' Championship in 2024 — its first since 1998 — and retained it in 2025, when Lando Norris won his first Drivers' Championship, ending Verstappen's four-year reign.[^a23] Lewis Hamilton left Mercedes for Ferrari ahead of the 2025 season after twelve years with the team.[^a23] The 2026 regulations close the ground-effect era introduced in 2022, reduce downforce by approximately fifteen per cent, retire the drag-reduction system in favour of an active-aerodynamics package, raise the share of power generated electrically to roughly fifty per cent, mandate fully sustainable fuels, and introduce a Manual Override Mode that allows trailing drivers to deploy additional electrical energy for overtaking.[^a24]

## Closing (Variant D — by impact on the sport)

Several drivers are credited with shaping the championship beyond their statistical records. Juan Manuel Fangio established the template of the modern multi-team champion in the 1950s.[^a4] Jim Clark's death in 1968 catalysed the first generation of professional safety reform.[^a10] Jackie Stewart converted that reform into a sustained driver-led campaign for crash barriers, seat belts, and the abandonment of unsafe circuits.[^a11] Niki Lauda's near-fatal Nürburgring crash in 1976 and the deaths of Ayrton Senna and Roland Ratzenberger at Imola in 1994 each produced regulatory overhauls that defined the modern car.[^a16] Michael Schumacher and Lewis Hamilton hold the all-time records for World Drivers' Championships at seven each; Max Verstappen claimed four consecutive titles from 2021 to 2024.[^a9] Among constructors, Ferrari's continuous presence since 1950 and Mercedes' eight consecutive Constructors' Championships from 2014 to 2021 are the longest unbroken commitment and longest dominance run in the sport's history.[^a2][^a5]
